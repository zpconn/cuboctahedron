\title{The Cuboctahedron Has No Nonsingular Omnihedral Periodic Billiard Orbit}
\author{Zach Conn}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We prove that the cuboctahedron has no nonsingular periodic billiard orbit
which visits each face exactly once in one period.  The proof uses the
classical unfolding construction: a billiard trajectory with a prescribed face
itinerary is replaced by a straight line in a reflected development of the
polyhedron.  This converts the problem into an exact finite obstruction
problem for affine reflection products.  The non-translation products are
ruled out by forced-axis obstructions.  The translation products reduce to
systems of strict linear inequalities in two variables and are ruled out by
Farkas certificates, positive-denominator obstructions, and Walsh
sign-polynomial families.  The exhaustive finite verification is large, and
the authoritative proof of that verification is the accompanying Lean
formalization in the GitHub repository.  The final compressed strategy was
shaped in part by source-size, memory, and build-time constraints.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The motion of a billiard particle in a polyhedron is simple locally and subtle
globally.  The particle moves along straight line segments, and whenever it
hits a face it reflects by the usual equal-angle law.  Singular hits at edges
or vertices are excluded in the present paper.  We study the following
face-covering question.

\begin{definition}
Let \(P\subset\R^3\) be a polyhedron with face set \(\F\).  A periodic billiard
orbit in \(P\) is \emph{omnihedral} if, during one period, it meets every face
of \(P\) exactly once.  It is \emph{nonsingular} if every impact point lies in
the relative interior of the face it meets.
\end{definition}

The cuboctahedron has fourteen faces: six squares and eight triangles.  It is
one of the Archimedean solids, and may be obtained by rectifying either the
cube or the octahedron.  Its symmetry makes it a natural test object for
questions about highly constrained billiard itineraries.  The main result is a
negative one.

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem]
The cuboctahedron has no nonsingular periodic billiard orbit which meets each
of its fourteen faces exactly once in one period.
\end{theorem}

The result should be viewed against the broader background of polygonal and
polyhedral billiards.  In rational polygonal billiards, the unfolding
construction leads to translation surfaces and a rich theory of periodic
directions; see for example Masur's theorem on closed trajectories
\cite{masur} and the density result of Boshernitzan, Galperin, Kruger, and
Troubetzkoy \cite{boshernitzan}.  By contrast, even elementary-looking
questions about periodic trajectories can remain difficult: the existence of a
periodic billiard orbit in every triangle is a famous open problem.

In higher-dimensional polyhedra the situation is still less rigidly organized.
The singular set is a graph rather than a finite set of vertices, unfoldings
develop in three-dimensional space, and the usual compact translation-surface
picture is no longer available.  Work such as that of Cekic, Georgiev, and
Mukherjee on polyhedral billiards and almost periodic control illustrates that
even avoiding neighborhoods of the singular set can require delicate analysis
\cite{cekic-georgiev-mukherjee}.

The omnihedral condition imposes a different kind of rigidity.  It asks for a
periodic trajectory that gives a minimal tour of the face set.  Such
trajectories are not unreasonable in highly symmetric settings: Bedaride and
Rao prove that the regular \(n\)-simplex has a period \(n+1\) billiard orbit
meeting each face once \cite{bedaride-rao}.  The cuboctahedron is close in
spirit to the Platonic solids, but has two face types.  The theorem says that
this mixture is already enough to obstruct an omnihedral orbit.

\section{The Cuboctahedron}

We use the following rational coordinate model.  Let
\[
P=\{(x,y,z)\in\R^3:
 |x|\le 1,\ |y|\le 1,\ |z|\le 1,\
 \pm x\pm y\pm z\le 2\}.
\]
The square faces are
\[
X_\pm:\ \pm x=1,\qquad
Y_\pm:\ \pm y=1,\qquad
Z_\pm:\ \pm z=1,
\]
and the triangular faces are
\[
T_{\epsilon_1\epsilon_2\epsilon_3}:
\epsilon_1x+\epsilon_2y+\epsilon_3z=2,
\qquad \epsilon_i\in\{\pm1\}.
\]
Thus the face set has \(6+8=14\) elements.

Each face \(F\) lies in a plane
\[
n_F\dotp p=c_F
\]
with \(n_F\in\Q^3\) and \(c_F\in\Q\).  Reflection in this plane is the affine
map
\[
r_F(p)
  =p-2\,\frac{n_F\dotp p-c_F}{n_F\dotp n_F}\,n_F.
\]
All coefficients in these reflection maps are rational.

\begin{definition}
Two faces are \emph{opposite} if they have parallel supporting planes and
opposite outward normals.  The cuboctahedron has seven opposite-face pairs:
three square pairs and four triangular pairs.
\end{definition}

The opposite-face pairs are useful because opposite faces have the same linear
reflection part.  They differ only in the affine translation term.

\section{Unfolding}

We recall the standard unfolding construction in the special form needed here.
Let
\[
F_0,F_1,\ldots,F_{13}
\]
be a proposed ordered list of faces.  Reflect the polyhedron successively in
these faces.  The original billiard path in \(P\) becomes a straight line in
the reflected development.  The product of the fourteen affine reflections is
an affine map
\[
A(p)=Mp+b,
\]
where \(M\in\operatorname{O}(3)\) and \(b\in\R^3\).  In the cuboctahedron
model above, \(M\) and \(b\) are rational whenever the faces are specified.

\begin{lemma}[Unfolding criterion]
Suppose a nonsingular billiard orbit in \(P\) meets the faces
\(F_0,\ldots,F_{13}\) in that cyclic order.  Then there is a straight line
\[
\ell(t)=p+tv
\]
in the unfolded development such that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \(\ell\) crosses the unfolded copy of \(F_i\) at the \(i\)-th impact;
\item every crossing lies in the relative interior of the corresponding face;
\item the affine return map \(A\) carries the line to itself and preserves its
  direction, so \(Mv=v\).
\end{enumerate}
Conversely, such an unfolded straight-line witness folds to a nonsingular
billiard orbit with the given itinerary.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
At each impact, instead of reflecting the velocity vector across the face, one
reflects the next copy of the polyhedron across that face.  The reflected
outgoing segment becomes the continuation of the incoming segment.  Repeating
this at all impacts turns the broken billiard trajectory into one straight
line.  Nonsingularity is exactly the assertion that the crossing points remain
in relative face interiors.  Periodicity says that after the fourteen
reflections the developed line is identified with its translate by the affine
return map; hence the direction is fixed by the linear part.  The construction
is reversible because reflecting the copies back folds the straight line into
a path satisfying the equal-angle reflection law.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Starting-face normalization]
If a nonsingular omnihedral period-14 orbit exists in the cuboctahedron, then
one exists whose first face is \(X_+\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
An omnihedral period contains every face exactly once, so some cyclic shift of
the period begins on \(X_+\).  Cyclically reindexing a periodic orbit does not
change the geometric path, its nonsingularity, or the one-hit-per-face
property.
\end{proof}

Thus it is enough to rule out started unfolded witnesses, meaning unfolded
straight-line witnesses whose first face is \(X_+\).

\section{The Finite Search Space}

After fixing \(F_0=X_+\), we first forget signs and remember only opposite-face
pairs.  The remaining thirteen entries must contain:
\[
\text{the }X\text{-pair once},\qquad
\text{the }Y\text{- and }Z\text{-pairs twice each},
\]
and each of the four triangular opposite-face pairs twice.  Hence the number
of opposite-pair words is
\[
\frac{13!}{2^6}=97{,}297{,}200.
\]

\begin{definition}
A \emph{pair word} is such a length-13 word in the seven opposite-face pairs,
after the initial face \(X_+\) has been fixed.  A \emph{signed lift} of a pair
word is a choice of the actual face in each opposite pair, compatible with the
requirement that every face is used exactly once.
\end{definition}

For a fixed pair word \(w\), the linear part \(M(w)\) of the return map depends
only on \(w\), not on the signed lift, because opposite faces have the same
linear reflection.  This gives the basic dichotomy.

\begin{definition}
A pair word is of \emph{non-translation type} if \(M(w)\ne I\).  It is of
\emph{translation type} if \(M(w)=I\).
\end{definition}

The finite enumeration has the following sizes:
\[
\begin{array}{lr}
\text{pair words} & 97{,}297{,}200,\\
\text{non-translation pair words} & 94{,}829{,}112,\\
\text{translation pair words} & 2{,}468{,}088.
\end{array}
\]
In the translation case there are six independent sign choices after the
started face is fixed, so the number of signed translation cases is
\[
2{,}468{,}088\cdot 64=157{,}957{,}632.
\]

\section{The Non-Translation Case}

Assume \(M\ne I\).  If an unfolded line \(\ell(t)=p+tv\) is compatible with
the affine return map \(A(p)=Mp+b\), then \(Mv=v\).  Thus \(v\) lies in the
fixed subspace of \(M\).  Moreover, the line itself is constrained by the
condition that \(A\ell=\ell\).  For the reflection products arising here, this
forces a unique axis or proves that no such axis exists.

\begin{lemma}[Forced-axis lemma]
Let \(A(p)=Mp+b\) be the return map of a started itinerary with \(M\ne I\).
If the itinerary has an unfolded periodic straight-line witness, then the
direction of that witness is a nonzero fixed vector of \(M\), and the starting
point lies on the affine axis determined by \(A\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By the unfolding criterion the return map carries the developed line to itself
and preserves the direction, so \(Mv=v\).  The condition \(A(p+tv)\in
\{p+sv:s\in\R\}\) for all \(t\) is equivalent to
\[
(I-M)p=b-\lambda v
\]
for some real \(\lambda\).  Once a fixed direction \(v\) is specified, this is
a linear system for \(p\) and \(\lambda\).  Therefore either no compatible
axis exists, or all compatible starting points lie on the affine solution set
of this system.  In the non-identity cases of the cuboctahedron enumeration,
the relevant solution set is either empty or the forced axis used in the
certificate verification.
\end{proof}

The forced axis must still pass several geometric tests.  It must meet the
relative interior of \(X_+\), cross subsequent unfolded faces from the correct
side, hit the prescribed face before any other face, and remain in the
relative interiors of the prescribed faces.

\begin{proposition}[Non-translation finite verification]
No started unfolded omnihedral witness realizes a non-translation pair word.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each non-translation pair word, the finite verification supplies one of
the following rational certificates:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item the fixed subspace of \(M\) contains no nonzero direction compatible with
  a line witness;
\item the forced axis does not meet the relative interior of \(X_+\);
\item the forced crossing direction has the wrong sign at some impact;
\item the forced signed lift is not omnihedral;
\item the first hit in the developed polyhedron is not the prescribed face;
\item a prescribed impact point lies outside the relative interior of its face.
\end{enumerate}
Each certificate is checked by rational linear algebra and rational strict
inequality arithmetic.  The forced-axis lemma shows that these certificates
cover all possible unfolded witnesses for the corresponding pair word.  The
finite list covers all \(94{,}829{,}112\) non-translation pair words.
\end{proof}

\section{The Translation Case}

Assume \(M=I\).  Then the affine return map is a translation
\[
A(p)=p+b.
\]
When \(b\ne 0\), an invariant unfolded line must have direction parallel to
\(b\).  Degenerate cases with \(b=0\), if they occur in the enumeration, are
included in the same finite verification below.  For the nondegenerate
translation cases, the starting point on \(X_+\) has the form
\[
p_0=(1,y,z).
\]
For a fixed signed itinerary, all crossing and face-interior conditions become
strict linear inequalities in the two variables \(y,z\).

\subsection{Positive denominators}

Let \(H_i\) be the \(i\)-th unfolded face plane, written
\[
u_i\dotp p=d_i.
\]
The time at which the line \(p_0+tb\) reaches this plane is
\[
t_i=\frac{d_i-u_i\dotp p_0}{u_i\dotp b}.
\]
For the internal crossings to occur in the prescribed forward order, the
denominators \(u_i\dotp b\) must have the prescribed positive sign.

\begin{lemma}[Positive-denominator condition]
Every feasible translation-type unfolded witness has
\[
u_i\dotp b>0
\]
for each internal impact \(i=1,\ldots,13\), after the itinerary has been
oriented by the started face.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The crossing times increase along the oriented unfolded line.  In the chosen
normalization, the numerator records the signed distance from the current
point to the next unfolded face plane, and the denominator records the rate at
which the line crosses that plane.  A nonpositive denominator would either
prevent the line from reaching the required plane in forward time or make the
crossing occur from the wrong side.  Thus each internal denominator is
positive.
\end{proof}

Consequently, any signed itinerary with a nonpositive internal denominator is
impossible before considering the remaining face-interior inequalities.

\subsection{Linear inequalities}

After the positive-denominator conditions are imposed, the statement that the
\(i\)-th impact lies in the relative interior of the required face is a finite
collection of inequalities of the form
\[
a y + c z + e >0,
\qquad a,c,e\in\Q.
\]
The starting point conditions on \(X_+\), the ordering conditions, and the
interior conditions at all subsequent impacts are all of this form.  Thus each
signed translation case gives a strict rational linear system in \((y,z)\).

\begin{definition}
A strict rational two-variable system is a finite set
\[
\mathcal S=\{a_j y+c_j z+e_j>0:1\le j\le N\},
\qquad a_j,c_j,e_j\in\Q.
\]
It is feasible if there are real numbers \(y,z\) satisfying every inequality.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Farkas certificate for strict two-variable systems]
Let \(\mathcal S\) be as above.  Suppose there are rational numbers
\(q_j\ge 0\), not all zero, such that
\[
\sum_j q_j a_j=0,\qquad
\sum_j q_j c_j=0,\qquad
\sum_j q_j e_j\le 0.
\]
Then \(\mathcal S\) is infeasible.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(y,z\) satisfied all inequalities, then for every \(j\) with \(q_j>0\),
\[
q_j(a_j y+c_j z+e_j)>0,
\]
and for every \(j\) with \(q_j=0\) the corresponding contribution is zero.
Since at least one multiplier is positive, summing gives
\[
0<\sum_j q_j(a_j y+c_j z+e_j)
  =\Big(\sum_j q_j a_j\Big)y
   +\Big(\sum_j q_j c_j\Big)z
   +\sum_j q_j e_j
  \le 0,
\]
a contradiction.
\end{proof}

Many signed translation cases are ruled out by certificates involving only two
rows of the system.  In such a case, the certificate records the two geometric
sources of the rows and a rational identity showing that the two inequalities
combine as in the lemma.  Families of cases often share the same row pattern,
even when the numerical constants vary.

\subsection{Sign-polynomial families}

There is another useful structure in the translation branch.  For a fixed pair
word, the six remaining sign choices may be encoded by variables
\[
s_1,\ldots,s_6\in\{1,-1\}.
\]
A function of these sign choices can be expanded in the Walsh basis: constants,
single signs \(s_i\), products \(s_i s_j\), and higher products.  Equivalently,
it is a multilinear polynomial on the Boolean cube \(\{\pm1\}^6\).  In this
problem, the denominator functions needed for the positive-denominator
condition have degree at most two.

\begin{lemma}[Impact denominators are Walsh polynomials]
Fix a translation-type pair word.  For each internal impact \(i\), the
corresponding denominator
\[
D_i(s_1,\ldots,s_6)=u_i(s_1,\ldots,s_6)\dotp b(s_1,\ldots,s_6)
\]
is a rational Walsh polynomial of degree at most two.  That is, there are
rational coefficients \(\alpha_i,\beta_{ij},\gamma_{ijk}\) such that
\[
D_i(s_1,\ldots,s_6)
  =\alpha_i+\sum_{j=1}^6 \beta_{ij}s_j
   +\sum_{1\le j<k\le 6}\gamma_{ijk}s_js_k .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The copied normal \(u_i\) of an unfolded impact plane depends affinely on the
sign choices: it is a constant vector plus a rational linear combination of
the sign variables.  The translation vector \(b\) has the same affine
dependence.  Their dot product is therefore the dot product of two affine
sign-vector expressions, and hence has degree at most two.  Since each
variable satisfies \(s_j^2=1\) on the sign cube, repeated variables reduce to
constants, leaving exactly the Walsh expansion displayed above.
\end{proof}

A subcube is obtained by fixing some of the signs and leaving the rest free.
If a quadratic sign polynomial is nonpositive on a subcube, then every signed
itinerary in that subcube violates the positive-denominator condition.

\begin{lemma}[Subcube obstruction]
Let \(D(s_1,\ldots,s_6)\) be an internal denominator.  If
\[
D(s_1,\ldots,s_6)\le 0
\]
for every sign assignment in a subcube, then no feasible unfolded witness has
a sign mask in that subcube.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is immediate from the positive-denominator condition.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Weighted Walsh subcube certificates}

The preceding subcube obstruction is useful, but in practice a slightly more
flexible version compresses better.  Instead of asking one denominator to be
nonpositive on a subcube, one may use a nonnegative linear combination of
several denominators.

\begin{lemma}[Weighted denominator subcube obstruction]
Fix a translation-type pair word, and let
\[
  D_1,\ldots,D_{13}:\{\pm1\}^6\to\Q
\]
be the internal impact denominators.  Let \(C\subset\{\pm1\}^6\) be a subcube.
Suppose there are rational weights \(\lambda_i\ge 0\), not all zero, such that
\[
  W(s)=\sum_{i=1}^{13}\lambda_iD_i(s)\le 0
\]
for every \(s\in C\).  Then no feasible translation witness has sign mask
lying in \(C\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For a feasible translation witness all internal denominators have the required
strict positive sign, so \(D_i(s)>0\) for every \(i\).  Since the weights are
nonnegative and at least one is positive, this gives
\[
  \sum_i\lambda_iD_i(s)>0.
\]
This contradicts the assumed inequality \(W(s)\le0\) on the subcube.
\end{proof}

The weighted sum \(W\) is again a rational Walsh polynomial of degree at most
two.  A very small certificate can prove that \(W\le0\) on a subcube after the
fixed signs have been substituted.

\begin{lemma}[Reduced Walsh bound]
Let \(C\) be a subcube of \(\{\pm1\}^6\).  After substituting the signs fixed
by \(C\), suppose a Walsh polynomial \(W\) reduces on the remaining free signs
to
\[
  W_{\mathrm{red}}(t_1,\ldots,t_m)
    = a_\varnothing+\sum_{\varnothing\ne A\subseteq\{1,\ldots,m\}}
      a_A\prod_{j\in A}t_j,
  \qquad t_j\in\{\pm1\}.
\]
If
\[
  a_\varnothing+\sum_{\varnothing\ne A}|a_A|\le0,
\]
then \(W(s)\le0\) for every \(s\in C\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For every free sign assignment, each monomial \(\prod_{j\in A}t_j\) is either
\(1\) or \(-1\).  Hence
\[
  a_A\prod_{j\in A}t_j\le |a_A|
\]
for every nonconstant monomial.  Summing these inequalities gives
\[
  W_{\mathrm{red}}(t_1,\ldots,t_m)
    \le a_\varnothing+\sum_{\varnothing\ne A}|a_A|\le0.
\]
Since \(W_{\mathrm{red}}\) agrees with \(W\) on the subcube, the claim follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[A typical reduced certificate]
The reduced-bound certificate is often much smaller than the unreduced Walsh
polynomial from which it came.  In the enumeration used by the finite
verification, the translation pair word with enumeration rank \(r=896\) has a
subcube described, in the bit order
\[
  y,\ z,\ d_{111},\ d_{11\bar{1}},\ d_{1\bar{1}1},\ d_{\bar{1}11},
\]
by the pattern
\[
  1**0*0.
\]
That is, \(y=1\), \(d_{11\bar{1}}=-1\), and
\(d_{\bar{1}11}=-1\), while the other three signs are free.  For this subcube,
the weighted denominator
\[
  W=D_2+D_6
\]
reduces to the one-variable expression
\[
  W_{\mathrm{red}}=-4+4z.
\]
Its reduced Walsh bound is
\[
  -4+|4|=0,
\]
so \(W\le0\) on the whole subcube.  By the weighted denominator obstruction,
no feasible translation witness can have a sign mask in this subcube.

In one representative finite certificate family, \(90\) of \(91\) selected
weighted subcubes were discharged directly by the reduced bound above, and
the remaining subcube was discharged after fixing one additional sign.  The
largest original weighted Walsh polynomial in that family had \(12\) nonzero
terms; after substitution, the largest reduced polynomial had \(6\) nonzero
terms and degree at most \(2\).  These figures are not used as axioms; they
only describe the compressed certificate surface that the Lean formalization
checks exactly.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Translation finite verification]
No started unfolded omnihedral witness realizes a translation-type signed
itinerary.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The finite verification considers every translation-type pair word and every
one of its \(64\) sign masks.  For each signed itinerary it supplies one of
the following rational obstructions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item an internal denominator is nonpositive;
\item a subcube sign-polynomial certificate proves that either an internal
  denominator, or a nonnegative weighted sum of internal denominators, is
  nonpositive for the whole family containing the sign mask;
\item a Farkas certificate proves that the associated strict linear system in
  \(y,z\) is infeasible;
\item a two-row template certificate proves the same infeasibility by naming
  the two geometric inequalities whose nonnegative rational combination gives
  a contradiction.
\end{enumerate}
The soundness of these certificate types is given by the preceding lemmas.
The exhaustive certificate list covers all \(157{,}957{,}632\) signed
translation cases.
\end{proof}

\section{Completion of the Proof}

\begin{theorem}
There is no started unfolded omnihedral witness for the cuboctahedron.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let a started unfolded omnihedral witness be given, and let \(w\) be its
opposite-face pair word.  If \(M(w)\ne I\), the non-translation finite
verification rules it out.  If \(M(w)=I\), the witness determines one of the
six-bit signed lifts of \(w\), and the translation finite verification rules
that out.  These two cases exhaust all pair words.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of the main theorem]
Suppose, for contradiction, that the cuboctahedron has a nonsingular
omnihedral periodic billiard orbit.  By the starting-face normalization, cyclic
reindexing gives such an orbit beginning on \(X_+\).  By the unfolding
criterion, this orbit gives a started unfolded omnihedral witness.  The
previous theorem says that no such witness exists, a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section{Comments on the Exhaustive Verification}

The proof above contains a large finite theorem: every admissible itinerary is
covered by one of the rational obstruction certificates.  The point of the
accompanying proof artifact is to make this theorem checkable rather than
trusting a numerical search.

Only exact arithmetic is used.  The reflections, affine products, rank
enumeration, sign choices, denominator tests, Farkas multipliers, and
sign-polynomial bounds all have rational coefficients.  There are no
floating-point tolerances or numerical approximations in the finite
verification.

The exhaustive verification has three conceptual layers:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item a bijective enumeration of the \(97{,}297{,}200\) pair words;
\item soundness proofs for each type of rational obstruction certificate;
\item a coverage proof showing that every pair word, and every required sign
  mask in the translation case, is assigned a sound obstruction.
\end{enumerate}
This is the part for which the GitHub formalization is the real proof.  The
printed article explains why such certificates prove the theorem; the
formalization checks that the certificates exist for every finite case.

\subsection*{Resource pressure as a source of structure}

One lesson of the formalization is that the computational constraints were not
merely an implementation nuisance.  They became a source of mathematical
structure.  The most direct proof-search strategies produced perfectly
reasonable local obstructions: one can ask a program to inspect a case, find a
certificate, and move on.  Logically, many of these early ideas were adequate.
Practically, they did not scale to a Lean proof.  They either emitted far too
much raw source code, or forced Lean to elaborate and replay proof terms whose
memory use and wall-clock cost were prohibitive.  Some early extrapolations of
one angle of attack led to running-time estimates on the order of thousands of
years.

At first, when the search was not seriously resource bounded, it naturally
found the simplest certificates that worked case by case.  Tightening the
memory and time budgets changed the nature of the search.  It made a merely
correct obstruction unsatisfactory unless it also compressed, reused, and
compiled.  Those constraints forced the search toward families, semantic
classifiers, source-indexed row relations, sign-polynomial subcube
obstructions, and other proof surfaces that were not apparent from the raw
geometry alone.

In that sense, the eventual strategy was not obtained by first discovering a
small proof and then implementing it.  The proof architecture emerged through
many rounds of failed but informative attempts, each ruled out by source size,
RAM usage, or build time.  The useful ideas then began to compound: a
denominator obstruction became more valuable once expressed over subcubes; a
two-row Farkas contradiction became more valuable once indexed by geometric
sources; a classifier became more valuable once it avoided even mentioning the
cases already eliminated by the positive-denominator theorem.  The resource
bounds acted as a kind of experimental pressure, turning proof search from a
large enumeration of facts into a search for reusable mathematical language.

\subsection*{Experimental theorem surfaces}

There is consequently a useful distinction between two kinds of theorems in
the formal proof artifact.  The first kind consists of the structural
soundness results: the unfolding criterion, the rank enumeration, the Farkas
certificate theorem, the positive-denominator condition, and the Walsh
subcube obstruction principle.  These are ordinary mathematical lemmas with
short conceptual proofs.

The second kind consists of generated or semi-generated theorem surfaces whose
statements record patterns extracted from the exact formal computation.  Such
theorems are still fully checked by Lean, but their purpose is different.
They need not provide a compact human explanation for why a particular list of
subcubes, row sources, or family descriptors is the right list.  Rather, they
assert that the extracted list is sound for its stated range or family, and
the proof is an exact replay of the relevant arithmetic, symbolic equalities,
and certificate checks.

Examples include the following.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item Generated Walsh-subcube cover theorems: for a fixed translation rank,
  a collection of Boolean subcubes and quadratic Walsh polynomials proves that
  all masks outside a specified good-mask set violate a positive-denominator
  condition.  The general Walsh bound theorem is conceptual; the particular
  subcubes, coefficients, and covering list are extracted data.
\item Source-index/state classifier theorems: a generated family descriptor
  may say that two named geometric constraint sources, together with one
  row-template relation, produce a two-source Farkas contradiction for every
  rank and mask matching that descriptor.  The soundness of the row relation
  is conceptual; the catalog of descriptors is an experimental compression of
  many individual cases.
\item Non-identity parametric-family theorems: intervals of pair-word ranks
  may be covered by certificate-producing functions with a common failure
  type, such as a forced direction or forced-axis obstruction.  These theorems
  are certified summaries of formal case data, not a hand classification of
  all geometric mechanisms in the non-identity branch.
\end{enumerate}

The Walsh good-mask sets also expose a small but definite structural pattern.
This pattern is not needed for the proof of nonexistence, but it is a useful
example of an experimental theorem surface that can be stated without
appealing to numerical approximation.

\begin{theorem}[Good-mask profile in the compact Walsh cover family]
Let
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal R=\{&
0,2,3,5,9,11,17,24,27,29,30,32,40,42,44,45,47,49,\\
&57,59,60,62,65,72,78,80,84,86,6000480,6000625,\\
&6000662,6000720,6000745\}.
\end{aligned}
\]
For each \(r\in\mathcal R\), let \(G_r\subset\{0,\ldots,63\}\) be the
good-mask set appearing in the generated compact-Walsh cover theorem for rank
\(r\).  Write the six mask bits in the order
\[
  y,\ z,\ d_{111},\ d_{11\bar{1}},\ d_{1\bar{1}1},\ d_{\bar{1}11},
\]
where the first two bits correspond to the two noninitial square-pair signs
and the last four bits correspond to the triangular opposite-face pairs.
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \(6\le |G_r|\le 16\) for every \(r\in\mathcal R\), and
  \[
    \sum_{r\in\mathcal R}|G_r|=379.
  \]
\item Among these \(379\) good-mask occurrences, the numbers of true bits in
  the six positions above are respectively
  \[
    180,\ 180,\ 257,\ 261,\ 263,\ 118.
  \]
\item If \(m\in G_r\) and the bit \(d_{\bar{1}11}\) of \(m\) is true, then at
  least two of the three bits
  \[
    d_{111},\quad d_{11\bar{1}},\quad d_{1\bar{1}1}
  \]
  are true.
\item The all-true mask lies in \(G_r\) for every \(r\in\mathcal R\) except
  \(r=6000480\).  The subcube
  \[
    d_{111}=d_{11\bar{1}}=d_{1\bar{1}1}=\text{true},
    \qquad d_{\bar{1}11}=\text{false}
  \]
  intersects \(G_r\) for \(31\) of the \(33\) ranks in \(\mathcal R\), and is
  contained in \(G_r\) for \(10\) of them.
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, for each \(r\in\mathcal R\), the generated compact-Walsh theorem
proves that every sign mask at rank \(r\) satisfying the positive-denominator
condition lies in \(G_r\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each listed rank, the generated Lean module defines \(G_r\) by an explicit
finite disjunction of mask values.  The cardinality, bit-count, all-true-mask,
and subcube-intersection assertions are obtained by decomposing these integers
in base two using the displayed bit order.  The triangular support implication
is the same finite check: none of the listed masks has \(d_{\bar{1}11}\) true
while fewer than two of \(d_{111},d_{11\bar{1}},d_{1\bar{1}1}\) are true.

The final sentence is the semantic part of the generated cover theorem.  Each
rank-local compact-Walsh module proves that a mask satisfying
\(\texttt{GoodDirectionAtRank}\) belongs to its displayed \(G_r\); equivalently,
every mask outside \(G_r\) is eliminated by one of the certified
Walsh-subcube positive-denominator obstructions.
\end{proof}

Thus, in this cover family, the fourth triangular pair
\(d_{\bar{1}11}\) appears as an exceptional sign: it survives only when
supported by at least two of the other three triangular signs.  The two square
signs are nearly balanced across the same data, while three of the triangular
bits are strongly biased toward truth and \(d_{\bar{1}11}\) is strongly biased
toward falsity.  This does not constitute a classification of all possible
translation ranks, but it is a precise extracted theorem about the
certified compact-Walsh cover surface currently used in the proof artifact.

This distinction does not weaken the proof.  In the final argument, only the
checked statements and their proved soundness matter.  It does, however,
describe how some of the mathematical structure was discovered.  The generated
experimental surfaces function like certified observations: they expose
regularities that were not initially visible as human-readable lemmas, and
some of those observations can later be promoted into cleaner reusable
theorem schemas.

\section{The Coherent Crossing Compatibility Conjecture}

The good-mask profile above suggests a possible general obstruction principle
for polyhedra whose faces come in opposite pairs.  The principle has two
parts.  The first is not conjectural: any nonsingular omnihedral orbit must
produce a direction satisfying a finite family of strict crossing
inequalities in the unfolded development.  The second is conjectural: in
highly organized opposite-pair polyhedra, the sign choices that can satisfy
these inequalities should be forced toward coherent side choices in the
visible opposite-pair families.

\begin{definition}[Positive crossing cone]
Let \(P\subset\R^3\) be a convex polyhedron with finite face set \(\F\), and
let
\[
  \sigma=(F_0,\ldots,F_{N-1})
\]
be an ordered list of faces.  Let
\[
  A_\sigma(x)=M_\sigma x+b_\sigma
\]
be the affine product of the reflections in these faces, with the same
unfolding convention as above.  For \(1\le i\le N-1\), let
\(u_i^\sigma\) be an oriented normal to the \(i\)-th unfolded impact plane,
chosen so that a legal crossing of that plane has positive denominator
\(u_i^\sigma\dotp v\).  The \emph{positive crossing cone} of \(\sigma\) is
\[
  C_\sigma
    =\{v\in\R^3:\ u_i^\sigma\dotp v>0
        \text{ for every }1\le i\le N-1\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Return-compatible directions]
For an itinerary \(\sigma\), define the return-direction set
\[
D_\sigma =
\begin{cases}
\{\lambda b_\sigma:\lambda>0\},
  & M_\sigma=I \text{ and } b_\sigma\ne0,\\
\R^3\setminus\{0\},
  & M_\sigma=I \text{ and } b_\sigma=0,\\
\ker(M_\sigma-I)\setminus\{0\},
  & M_\sigma\ne I.
\end{cases}
\]
We say that \(\sigma\) is \emph{positive-crossing compatible} if
\[
  D_\sigma\cap C_\sigma\ne\varnothing.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Positive crossing compatibility is necessary]
If \(P\) has a nonsingular periodic billiard orbit with face itinerary
\(\sigma\), then \(\sigma\) is positive-crossing compatible.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Unfold the orbit along \(\sigma\).  The billiard path becomes a straight line
\(\ell(t)=p+tv\).  Nonsingularity and the prescribed ordering of the impacts
imply that every internal crossing denominator is strictly positive, so
\(v\in C_\sigma\).  The affine return map carries the unfolded line to itself.
If \(M_\sigma\ne I\), this implies \(M_\sigma v=v\), hence
\(v\in D_\sigma\).  If \(M_\sigma=I\) and \(b_\sigma\ne0\), then one period of
the unfolded line advances by the translation \(b_\sigma\), so
\(b_\sigma=\lambda v\) for some \(\lambda>0\); replacing \(v\) by the positive
multiple \(b_\sigma\) shows that \(D_\sigma\cap C_\sigma\ne\varnothing\).  If
\(M_\sigma=I\) and \(b_\sigma=0\), the nonzero line direction itself lies in
the second case of \(D_\sigma\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Side coherence]
Suppose a collection \(\mathcal T\) of faces of \(P\) is organized into
opposite pairs \(p\).  For each pair choose a canonical normal \(n_p\), so the
two possible first choices in that pair have outward normals \(n_p\) and
\(-n_p\).  A sign choice may therefore be written as
\(s_p\in\{1,-1\}\), where the first face chosen from pair \(p\) has outward
normal \(s_p n_p\).

Let \(a\in\R^3\) satisfy \(n_p\dotp a\ne0\) for every pair
\(p\in\mathcal T\).  The sign choice is \emph{\(a\)-side coherent on
\(\mathcal T\)} if
\[
  s_p(n_p\dotp a)>0
  \qquad\text{for every }p\in\mathcal T.
\]
Equivalently, the first chosen faces in the family all lie on the same
normal side selected by \(a\).  This condition is independent of the arbitrary
choice of \(n_p\), since changing \(n_p\) to \(-n_p\) also changes \(s_p\) to
\(-s_p\).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Started coherence datum]
Let \(S\) be a starting face of a polyhedron \(P\), and let \(a_S\) be an
outward normal to \(S\).  The \emph{started coherence datum} associated to
\(S\) consists of the pairs \((\mathcal T,a_S)\) for which \(\mathcal T\) is
an orbit of opposite-face pairs not containing \(S\), under the symmetry
subgroup preserving \(S\), and no canonical normal in \(\mathcal T\) is
orthogonal to \(a_S\).

A signed omnihedral itinerary beginning with \(S\) is
\emph{\(S\)-coherent} if its sign choices are \(a_S\)-side coherent on every
family appearing in this datum.  It is \emph{coherently crossing compatible}
if it is \(S\)-coherent and positive-crossing compatible.
\end{definition}

For the cuboctahedron started at \(X_+\), the visible nonorthogonal family is
the four triangular opposite pairs.  Taking \(a_S=(1,0,0)\), side coherence
is exactly the subcube
\[
  d_{111}=d_{11\bar{1}}=d_{1\bar{1}1}=\text{true},
  \qquad d_{\bar{1}11}=\text{false}.
\]
Thus the subcube in the good-mask profile theorem has a geometric meaning:
the first triangle chosen from every triangular opposite pair lies on the
\(X_+\)-side.  The same theorem also shows why coherence alone is not an
existence criterion.  At rank \(6000480\), only one mask in that coherent
subcube survives the compact Walsh denominator filter, and the all-true mask
does not survive.  The proposed principle is therefore a compatibility
principle, not a statement that side coherence by itself should produce an
orbit.

\begin{conjecture}[Coherent Crossing Compatibility Conjecture]
Let \(P\) be a rational convex polyhedron whose faces are organized, after a
start face is chosen, into symmetry families of opposite pairs as above.  If
\(P\) admits a nonsingular omnihedral periodic billiard orbit, then there is
a cyclic shift of that orbit, beginning at some face \(S\), such that the
corresponding signed omnihedral itinerary is coherently crossing compatible:
its sign choices are \(S\)-coherent for the started coherence datum of \(S\),
and
\[
  D_\sigma\cap C_\sigma\ne\varnothing .
\]
Thus, contrapositively, if every \(S\)-coherent signed omnihedral itinerary
fails positive crossing compatibility, for every possible start face \(S\),
then \(P\) has no nonsingular omnihedral periodic billiard orbit.
\end{conjecture}

This conjecture is deliberately one-sided.  It proposes a general obstruction
test, not a sufficient condition for existence.  Even when coherent crossing
compatibility holds, one must still check the remaining relative-interior
inequalities for the unfolded impact points.  The cuboctahedron proof fits
the conjectural picture in the strongest possible negative way: the exact
finite verification shows that every started itinerary fails the full
unfolded feasibility problem, and the Walsh subcube data suggests that the
failure is organized around side-coherence constraints rather than around an
unstructured list of isolated sign masks.
