\begin{abstract}
We study whether a cuboctahedron admits a periodic billiard path that reflects
once from each of its fourteen faces during one period.  We call such a period
\emph{omnihedral}; we call it \emph{nonsingular} if every reflection point lies
in the relative interior of the face it meets, rather than on an edge or
vertex.  The mathematical target is to prove that no nonsingular omnihedral
period exists.

This article describes the exact formal proof architecture currently present
in a Lean 4 development.  Lean 4 is a proof assistant, and mathlib is its
standard mathematical library; in this project they are used to check every
geometric and finite-combinatorial step with rational and integer arithmetic.
As of this manuscript date, the public Lean theorem is conditional on one
remaining finite coverage object, by which we mean a Lean-checked proof that
every case in a finite case split has been assigned to a verified obstruction.
The already checked part proves that such coverage implies the desired
billiard theorem.

The main checked structural ingredient is a normal form for the linear part of
the return map, meaning the affine map obtained by composing the fourteen face
reflections in the unfolded picture.  After cyclically choosing \(X_+\) as the
first face, this normal form reduces every face order to a finite word in
opposite-face pairs, meaning pairs of parallel faces, and proves in Lean
exactly when the linear return map is the identity.  The identity case becomes
a two-variable rational linear infeasibility problem.  The nonidentity case
becomes a problem about an affine line forced by a nonidentity return map.  For
the hard family left after simpler obstructions in that branch, the latest
checked interface uses a deterministic finite-state
evaluator, also called a transducer: it reads the face sequence one face at a
time, carries the state needed to prove membership in the family, and projects
the result to a finite-state potential certificate.  We call such a potential
certificate a Bellman certificate when it consists of a finite graph with
integer transition gains and an integer potential whose local inequalities
telescope to a global obstruction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A billiard path in a polyhedron is a broken line that travels at constant
speed inside the polyhedron and reflects specularly from faces: the tangential
component of the velocity is preserved and the normal component is reversed.
The path is \emph{periodic} if, after finitely many reflections, it returns to
the same position and direction.  It is \emph{nonsingular} if no reflection
point lies on an edge or vertex, so the reflecting face is always uniquely
defined.  For the cuboctahedron, which has fourteen faces, we say that a
period is \emph{omnihedral} if the fourteen reflections in one period meet all
fourteen faces exactly once.

\begin{theorem}[Target theorem]
The cuboctahedron has no nonsingular omnihedral periodic billiard orbit.
\end{theorem}

The proof is being formalized in Lean 4.  A Lean theorem is a machine-checked
object in a small logical kernel, while a Lean definition can also be executed
or simplified during checking.  This distinction matters here because the
finite search space is large.  External programs are useful for finding
certificates, but they are not trusted as proof.  A generated file contributes
to the proof only when Lean checks a theorem whose statement is strong enough
to rule out the corresponding geometric cases.

Call a finite case split \emph{covered} when Lean has proved, for every case in
the split, that the case satisfies one of the checked obstruction predicates.
The current public theorem packages complete coverage in a Lean structure
named
\begin{center}
  \texttt{ExhaustiveGeneratedCoverage}.
\end{center}
The theorem now proved in Lean is:
\begin{verbatim}
theorem Cuboctahedron.conditional_cuboctahedron_no_omnihedral
    (coverage : ExhaustiveGeneratedCoverage) :
    Not (exists o : BilliardOrbit14,
      o.Nonsingular /\ o.Periodic /\ o.TouchesEachFaceExactlyOnce)
\end{verbatim}
Thus the geometric bridge from complete finite coverage to the billiard
theorem is already checked.  What remains is to construct the coverage object
by generated Lean modules, not by trusting a search script.

The paper is organized so that every project-specific term is introduced
before it is used.  Section~\ref{sec:model} gives the exact cuboctahedron and
reflection model.  Section~\ref{sec:words} explains the unfolding reduction
and the finite word space.  Section~\ref{sec:holonomy} proves the checked
normal form for the linear return map.  Sections~\ref{sec:nonidentity}
and~\ref{sec:translation} describe the two branches of the proof.  Finally,
Sections~\ref{sec:coverage} and~\ref{sec:validation} state precisely what is
trusted, what is generated, and what remains conditional in the present
repository state.

\section{The Exact Cuboctahedron Model}
\label{sec:model}

We use coordinates in \(\R^3\).  The cuboctahedron is the polyhedron
\[
P=\{(x,y,z)\in\R^3:
 |x|\le 1,\ |y|\le 1,\ |z|\le 1,\
 \epsilon_1x+\epsilon_2y+\epsilon_3z\le 2
 \text{ for all }\epsilon_i\in\{\pm1\}\}.
\]
It has six square faces,
\[
X_\pm:\ \pm x=1,\qquad
Y_\pm:\ \pm y=1,\qquad
Z_\pm:\ \pm z=1,
\]
and eight triangular faces,
\[
T_{\epsilon_1\epsilon_2\epsilon_3}:
\epsilon_1x+\epsilon_2y+\epsilon_3z=2,
\qquad \epsilon_i\in\{\pm1\}.
\]

Every face \(F\) is stored by a rational normal vector \(n_F\in\Q^3\) and a
rational offset \(c_F\in\Q\), so the supporting plane is
\[
  n_F\dotp p=c_F.
\]
Reflection in this plane is the affine map
\[
  r_F(p)=p-2\,\frac{n_F\dotp p-c_F}{n_F\dotp n_F}\,n_F.
\]
Here ``affine'' means that the map has the form
\[
  r_F(p)=L_Fp+d_F
\]
where \(L_F\) is a linear map and \(d_F\) is a translation vector.  Since all
normals and offsets are rational, every entry of \(L_F\) and \(d_F\) is
rational.  The formal proof uses only exact rational and integer arithmetic;
it uses no floating point numbers, numerical tolerances, or epsilon tests.

\section{From Billiards to Finite Words}
\label{sec:words}

The standard way to study a billiard path is to \emph{unfold} it.  Instead of
reflecting the velocity vector when the path hits a face, reflect the next
copy of the polyhedron across that face.  In the reflected copies the billiard
path becomes a straight line.  Conversely, a straight line through reflected
copies folds back to a billiard path when each crossing lies in the relative
interior of the prescribed face.

\begin{definition}[Face itinerary]
A \emph{face itinerary} of length \(14\) is a function
\[
  s:\{0,\ldots,13\}\to\F,
\]
where \(\F\) is the set of the fourteen faces.  The itinerary is
\emph{omnihedral} if it is a bijection.  It is \emph{started} if
\[
  s(0)=X_+.
\]
\end{definition}

For a face itinerary \(s\), define its unfolded return map by composing the
fourteen face reflections:
\[
  A_s=r_{s(13)}\circ\cdots\circ r_{s(0)}.
\]
Write
\[
  A_s(p)=M_sp+b_s
\]
for its linear part \(M_s\) and translation part \(b_s\).

\begin{definition}[Unfolded witness]
An \emph{unfolded witness} for \(s\) is a line
\[
  \ell(t)=p+tv,\qquad v\ne0,
\]
such that the developed line crosses the reflected faces in the order
prescribed by \(s\), every crossing lies in the relative interior of that
developed face, and the affine return map \(A_s\) carries the line to itself.
In particular, the direction satisfies
\[
  M_sv=v.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Unfolding criterion]
A nonsingular billiard orbit with face itinerary \(s\) exists if and only if
there is an unfolded witness for \(s\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Reflect the next copy of the cuboctahedron instead of reflecting the velocity
vector.  This makes the billiard segments collinear.  Reflection preserves
relative interiors of faces, so the nonsingularity condition is unchanged.
Periodicity is exactly the statement that the affine return map identifies the
final developed line with the initial one.  Folding the reflected copies back
reverses the construction.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Starting-face reduction]
If a nonsingular omnihedral period exists, then one exists whose itinerary is
started.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
An omnihedral period meets \(X_+\) exactly once.  Cyclically reindex the
period so that this reflection is first.
\end{proof}

The remaining finite reduction first forgets the signs of opposite faces.  Two
faces are in the same \emph{opposite-face pair} if their supporting planes are
parallel.  There are seven such pairs:
\[
x,\ y,\ z,\ d_{111},\ d_{11\bar 1},\ d_{1\bar 1 1},\
d_{\bar 1 11}.
\]
The first three are square-face pairs.  The last four are triangular-face
pairs, named by the projective normal direction of the pair.

\begin{definition}[Started pair word]
A \emph{started pair word} is the length-\(13\) word in the seven
opposite-face pairs obtained from a started itinerary after deleting the
initial face \(X_+\) and then replacing every remaining face by its
opposite-face pair.  It is \emph{valid} if the multiplicities are
\[
x:1,\qquad y:2,\qquad z:2,\qquad
d_{111},d_{11\bar 1},d_{1\bar 1 1},d_{\bar 1 11}:2.
\]
The finite set of valid started pair words is denoted by \(\W\).
\end{definition}

The number of valid started pair words is
\[
  |\W|=\frac{13!}{2^6}=97{,}297{,}200.
\]
Lean defines two functions, called \(\rank\) and \(\unrank\), that give a
lexicographic address for this finite set:
\[
  \unrank:\{0,\ldots,97{,}297{,}199\}\to\W.
\]
The word ``rank'' here means only an address in a finite list.  It is not a
matrix rank or a geometric invariant.  Lean proves that the address map and
its inverse enumerate exactly the valid started pair words.

In the branch where the linear return map is the identity, the affine
translation vector depends on which member of each opposite-face pair was
used.  Once the first face \(X_+\) is fixed, the remaining choices are encoded
by six independent bits.  We call these six bits a \emph{sign mask} and write
\[
  \Masks=\{\pm1\}^6.
\]

\section{The Linear Return Map and Its Normal Form}
\label{sec:holonomy}

The linear part \(M_s\) of the unfolded return map depends only on the started
pair word, not on the sign mask.  We write \(M(w)\) for this linear part.  The
Lean product convention is
\[
  R(w_0)R(w_1)\cdots R(w_{12})R(x),
\]
where \(R(\cdot)\) is the linear reflection associated to an opposite-face
pair, and the final factor \(R(x)\) comes from the initial face \(X_+\).

The word \emph{holonomy} is used here in a modest sense: it means the linear
effect obtained after transporting a direction through the full sequence of
face reflections.  A \emph{normal form} for this holonomy is a smaller exact
summary from which Lean can decide whether \(M(w)=I\).

\begin{definition}[Square parity]
The square-pair reflections \(x,y,z\) are diagonal sign changes.  Their
accumulated effect is recorded by
\[
  p=(p_x,p_y,p_z)\in(\Z/2\Z)^3.
\]
Scanning a square-pair letter toggles the corresponding coordinate of \(p\).
Scanning a triangular-pair letter leaves \(p\) unchanged.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Triangular shadow]
Let
\[
  \T=\{111,\ 11\bar 1,\ 1\bar 1 1,\ \bar 1 11\}
\]
be the four triangular projective normal directions.  When a triangular
letter is scanned after the current square parity \(p\), the square sign
changes act on it.  If the triangular letter is written projectively as
\((1,\eta,\zeta)\), then \(p\) sends it to
\[
  \bigl(1,\ (-1)^{p_x+p_y}\eta,\ (-1)^{p_x+p_z}\zeta\bigr).
\]
The \emph{triangular shadow} is the stack of acted triangular letters obtained
while scanning the pair word, with adjacent equal letters cancelled.  The
final reduced stack is denoted \(\red(w)\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Lean-checked holonomy classifier]
For every valid started pair word \(w\),
\[
  M(w)=I
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \red(w)=[].
\]
In the Lean repository this theorem is named
\begin{center}
{\small\ttfamily totalLinearOfPairWord\_eq\_identity\_iff}\\
{\small\ttfamily reducedShadow\_empty\_of\_valid}
\end{center}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof idea]
The scan maintains a product invariant: the accumulated pair-reflection
product factors as a triangular product times the current square-parity
matrix.  Valid pair-word counts imply that the final square parity is trivial
after the started \(x\) factor is included.

Adjacent equal triangular reflections square to the identity, so cancelling
adjacent equal letters does not change the triangular product.  It remains to
show that a nonempty reduced triangular word cannot have identity product.

For a triangular normal \(n\in\{\pm1\}^3\), the reflection matrix is
\[
  H_n=I-\frac{2}{3}nn^T.
\]
Scale it to the integer matrix
\[
  K_n=3H_n=3I-2nn^T.
\]
Modulo \(3\), one has \(K_n\equiv nn^T\).  A nonempty reduced triangular word
therefore gives, modulo \(3\), a product of linear-algebra rank-one matrices.
Since adjacent projective triangular letters are distinct, every successive
dot product is nonzero in \(\mathbb F_3\).  The product is a nonzero scalar
multiple of an outer product, hence is nonzero modulo \(3\).  If the rational
triangular product were \(I\), the scaled integer product of length \(m>0\)
would be \(3^mI\), which is zero modulo \(3\), a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
An exact external scan found \(2{,}468{,}088\) empty reduced shadows and
\(94{,}829{,}112\) nonempty reduced shadows.  These counts are useful
diagnostics, but the proof does not trust them.  The Lean theorem above is the
trusted identity/nonidentity split.
\end{remark}

\section{The Nonidentity Branch}
\label{sec:nonidentity}

The \emph{nonidentity branch} consists of valid started pair words with
\(\red(w)\ne[]\).  By the holonomy classifier, these are exactly the words
whose linear return map satisfies \(M(w)\ne I\).

Suppose a signed itinerary realizing such a word had an unfolded witness.  The
return map \(A(p)=Mp+b\) would carry the witness line to itself.  The line
direction \(v\) would satisfy \(Mv=v\), and the base point \(p_0\) would lie on
an affine line constrained by the following equation.

\begin{lemma}[Forced-axis equation]
Let \(A(p)=Mp+b\) with \(M\ne I\).  If \(A\) preserves the line
\(p_0+\R v\) and preserves its direction, then \(v\ne0\), \(Mv=v\), and there
is \(\lambda\in\R\) such that
\[
  (I-M)p_0=b-\lambda v.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The direction condition gives \(Mv=v\).  Since \(A(p_0)\) lies on the same
line, \(Mp_0+b=p_0+\lambda v\) for some \(\lambda\).  Rearranging gives the
displayed equation.
\end{proof}

The phrase \emph{forced axis} refers to the affine line, or affine family of
lines, described by this equation together with the eigenvector condition
\(Mv=v\).  Many nonidentity cases are ruled out immediately: there may be no
compatible nonzero fixed direction, the forced crossing direction may have the
wrong sign, the forced signed face sequence may fail to be omnihedral, the
forced axis may miss the relative interior of \(X_+\), or a required impact
may fall outside the intended face.

The remaining nonidentity cases are grouped by what we call
\emph{semantic families}: a semantic family is a mathematical predicate saying
that a face sequence has certain structural features.  It is not a list of
numerical ranks.  The current hard family in the repository is named the
\emph{top-pairing} family;
the name is only a label for one residual pattern in the triangular
cancellation data.  Its semantic predicate records:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item a fixed cancellation-pairing pattern in the triangular shadow;
\item an allowed-face schedule at each of the fourteen contribution steps;
\item a square-gap condition, meaning a restriction on where square faces may
  occur relative to triangular contributions;
\item local-axis sign tests, meaning exact rational positivity tests computed
  from prefix reflection matrices;
\item compatibility with a canonical bad face whose interior margin is being
  tested.
\end{enumerate}

A semantic family is called \emph{closed} when the information needed to check
membership can be advanced one face at a time without leaving the finite state
space assigned to the family.  This closure condition is what allows the
finite-state potential route in Section~\ref{sec:bellman} to avoid storing one
proof per rank.

\section{Finite-State Potential Certificates}
\label{sec:bellman}

This section gives the elementary finite-state potential argument used later.
Because it is the same kind of potential inequality that appears in Bellman's
work on routing and dynamic programming, we call a certificate of this form a
\emph{Bellman certificate}.  No analytic approximation is involved.

\begin{definition}[Bellman graph]
A Bellman graph consists of states \(S\), directed transitions
\[
  e=(s,g,t),\qquad s,t\in S,\quad g\in\Z,
\]
and a potential function
\[
  V:S\to\Z.
\]
The transition \(e=(s,g,t)\) is \(V\)-valid if
\[
  g+V(t)\le V(s).
\]
The integer \(g\) is called the gain of the transition.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Telescoping potential inequality]
If
\[
  s_0\xrightarrow{g_0}s_1\xrightarrow{g_1}\cdots
  \xrightarrow{g_{m-1}}s_m
\]
is a path whose transitions are all \(V\)-valid, then
\[
  \sum_{i=0}^{m-1}g_i+V(s_m)\le V(s_0).
\]
In particular, if \(V(s_m)\ge0\), then
\[
  \sum_{i=0}^{m-1}g_i\le V(s_0).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Sum the local inequalities \(g_i+V(s_{i+1})\le V(s_i)\).  The intermediate
potential terms cancel.
\end{proof}

In the geometric application, a forced-axis semantic family supplies an exact
rational margin \(\mu\).  The margin is chosen so that every feasible unfolded
witness in the family would force
\[
  \mu>0.
\]
After clearing denominators, Lean works with an integer-scaled margin.  A
Bellman certificate proves the opposite inequality, namely that every accepted
object in the semantic family has scaled margin at most \(0\).

\begin{proposition}[Family-level Bellman certificate]
Let \(X\) be a set of finite objects, each with a word of labels
\(\word(x)\).  Suppose there are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item a deterministic transition function that reads the labels of
  \(\word(x)\), accumulates integer gains, and either succeeds or fails;
\item a potential \(V:S\to\Z\);
\item a constant \(c\in\Z\);
\item an integer-scaled margin \(\mu:X\to\Z\).
\end{enumerate}
Here an \emph{accepted} object means an object satisfying the family predicate
being certified.  Assume that every accepted object \(x\) has a successful run
ending at \(s_f\) with total gain \(G\), that \(V(s_f)\ge0\), that
\[
  \mu(x)\le c+G,
\]
that every local transition satisfies \(g+V(t)\le V(s)\), and that the root
bound \(c+V(s_0)\le0\) holds.  Then every accepted object has
\[
  \mu(x)\le0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The telescoping lemma gives \(G\le V(s_0)\).  Hence
\[
  \mu(x)\le c+G\le c+V(s_0)\le0.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{The Latest Transducer Interface}

A deterministic finite-state evaluator that reads one input symbol at a time
and updates its state is often called a \emph{transducer}.  In this project,
the input symbols are faces, and the output accumulated along the run is the
integer Bellman gain.

The newest checked nonidentity interface adds a transducer state for the
top-pairing family.  This state stores:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item the current step number;
\item the current square-gap count;
\item a cursor into the target triangular shadow, meaning the fixed
  triangular-shadow pattern defining this family;
\item the current square parity;
\item the current exact linear reflection product.
\end{enumerate}
The predicate
\begin{center}
  \texttt{TopPairingTransducerTail}
\end{center}
means that this state is semantically consistent with the remaining suffix of
faces.  It packages the older schedule, square-gap, and local-axis suffix facts
together with the target-shadow cursor.

The Lean file \texttt{TopPairingTransducerEvalBridge.lean} proves two facts
intended for use by generated proof files.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item If every semantically valid head step has a graph transition, then the
  combined face evaluator succeeds on the whole semantic tail.
\item In the invariant-carrying version, generated code may maintain an
  additional finite invariant; Lean proves that local invariant-preserving
  transition totality is enough to prove that this rank has a successful
  Bellman evaluation.
\end{enumerate}
The key theorem names are
\begin{center}
{\small\ttfamily evalLabelStepFn\_exists\_of\_tail\_and\_invariantLocal}\\
{\small\ttfamily evalLanguageAtRank\_of\_invariantLocal}
\end{center}
This is a tighter proof boundary than a trace table.  A generated module no
longer needs to replay a few hand-picked prefixes or traces at particular rank
addresses.  It must prove local transition coverage for semantic states, and
Lean performs the recursive evaluation and projection to the ordinary Bellman
graph evaluator.

The repository also contains generated smoke tests for this interface.  A
``smoke test'' here means a small Lean module proving that the interface works
on a bounded example.  These tests are valuable for checking the shape of the
generated proof, but they are not exhaustive coverage.

\section{The Identity Branch}
\label{sec:translation}

The \emph{identity branch} consists of valid started pair words with
\(\red(w)=[]\).  By the holonomy classifier, these are exactly the words whose
linear return map is the identity.  For a signed itinerary in this branch, the
affine return map has the form
\[
  A(p)=p+b.
\]
It is therefore a translation by the vector \(b\).

For a nondegenerate periodic unfolded witness, the line direction must be
parallel to \(b\).  Since the first face is \(X_+\), write the starting point
as
\[
  p_0=(1,y,z).
\]
For a fixed pair word and sign mask, the face-interior and crossing-order
conditions become strict rational linear inequalities in the two variables
\(y\) and \(z\).

If the \(i\)-th developed face plane is
\[
  u_i\dotp p=d_i,
\]
then the crossing time along direction \(b\) is
\[
  t_i=\frac{d_i-u_i\dotp p_0}{u_i\dotp b}.
\]
The denominator \(D_i=u_i\dotp b\) must have the correct sign for a forward
crossing.

\begin{definition}[Good direction condition]
An identity-branch signed case satisfies the \emph{good direction condition}
if all internal crossing denominators \(D_i\) have the required positive sign
after the fixed orientation convention is chosen.  In the Lean code this
predicate is named \texttt{GoodDirection}.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Good direction is necessary]
If an identity-branch signed itinerary has an unfolded witness, then it
satisfies the good direction condition.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Each prescribed developed face must be crossed in forward time and from the
prescribed side.  With the fixed orientation, the numerator in the displayed
formula for \(t_i\) has the prescribed sign.  A nonpositive denominator would
reverse or degenerate that crossing.
\end{proof}

After the good direction condition is imposed, the remaining constraints have
the form
\[
  a_jy+c_jz+e_j>0
\]
with rational coefficients.  Lean checks infeasibility using the following
strict two-variable form of Farkas' lemma.

\begin{lemma}[Strict two-variable Farkas certificate]
Suppose nonnegative rational multipliers \(q_j\), not all zero, satisfy
\[
  \sum_j q_ja_j=0,\qquad
  \sum_j q_jc_j=0,\qquad
  \sum_j q_je_j\le0.
\]
Then there are no \(y,z\in\R\) satisfying all inequalities
\[
  a_jy+c_jz+e_j>0.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If all strict inequalities held, multiplying them by the nonnegative \(q_j\)
and summing would give a strict positive number.  The coefficient sums make
the \(y\) and \(z\) terms vanish, while the constant sum is nonpositive, a
contradiction.
\end{proof}

A generated Farkas certificate names the inequalities it uses.  The proof does
not trust a row merely because a script printed it.  Each row is reproduced in
Lean from a source such as a start-face condition, an ordering condition, or an
impact-face interior condition, and Lean checks the rational linear
combination.

\section{Generated Coverage}
\label{sec:coverage}

The word \emph{certificate} in this project means finite data together with a
Lean theorem or checker proving that the data imply a mathematical
obstruction.  The word \emph{coverage} means that every case in the finite
search space is assigned to some checked obstruction.

The public generated predicates are deliberately small.  The nonidentity
predicate is
\begin{center}
  \texttt{NonIdentityRankKilled r}.
\end{center}
It means: if rank \(r\) is in the nonidentity branch, then no started signed
itinerary realizing that rank has an unfolded witness.  The identity-branch
predicate is
\begin{center}
  \texttt{TranslationCaseKilled r mask}.
\end{center}
It means: if rank \(r\) is in the identity branch, then the signed translation
case indexed by the sign mask has no unfolded witness.

\begin{definition}[Semantic generated coverage]
Semantic generated coverage consists of:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item the Lean-checked rank and unrank address space for all
  \(97{,}297{,}200\) valid started pair words;
\item the Lean-checked \(64\)-element sign-mask address space;
\item a proof of \texttt{NonIdentityRankKilled r} for every rank \(r\);
\item a proof of \texttt{TranslationCaseKilled r mask} for every rank
  \(r\) and every sign mask.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Coverage kills started unfolded witnesses]
If semantic generated coverage holds, then no started omnihedral itinerary has
an unfolded witness.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let a started unfolded witness be given.  Forgetting signs gives a valid
started pair word \(w\), hence a rank \(r=\rank(w)\).  By the holonomy
classifier, either \(\red(w)\ne[]\) and the case is nonidentity-linear, or
\(\red(w)=[]\) and the case is identity-linear.

In the nonidentity case, \texttt{NonIdentityRankKilled r} contradicts the
witness.  In the identity case, the signed itinerary determines a sign mask
\(m\), and \texttt{TranslationCaseKilled r m} contradicts the witness.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Current formal theorem]
If \texttt{ExhaustiveGeneratedCoverage} is supplied, then the
cuboctahedron has no nonsingular omnihedral periodic billiard orbit.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume such an orbit exists.  Cyclically reindex it to start at \(X_+\).  The
unfolding criterion gives a started unfolded witness.  The previous
proposition, instantiated by the supplied coverage object, rules this out.
\end{proof}

residual bridge is the remaining nonidentity obligation, and the Farkas bridge
is the remaining translation-case rational infeasibility obligation.

ordinary Lean hypotheses in the assembly layer, not hidden assumptions.

\section{Verification Boundary}
\label{sec:validation}

The trusted part of the project is the Lean development, including:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item exact rational definitions of the cuboctahedron, faces, and reflection
  maps;
\item the unfolding criterion and started-itinerary reduction;
\item the rank and unrank enumeration theorem for valid pair words;
\item the holonomy normal form and identity classifier;
\item the good direction necessity theorem for translation cases;
\item the strict rational Farkas soundness theorem and row-source checkers;
\item the generic Bellman potential theorems;
\item the top-pairing semantic language, transducer-tail predicate, and
  deterministic evaluator bridge;
\item generated Lean modules to the extent that they prove the theorem-valued
  predicates consumed by the coverage interface.
\end{enumerate}

The untrusted part consists of profilers, search scripts, JSON files, markdown
reports, old C++ outputs, and bounded smoke tests.  These artifacts may suggest
certificate data or test the shape of a generator.  They become proof only when
Lean checks a theorem that consumes them.

For the current conditional proof surface, the intended repository checks are:
\begin{verbatim}
lake build
grep -R "sorry\|admit\|axiom\|native_decide\|unsafe" Cuboctahedron || true
lake env lean Cuboctahedron/PrintConditionalAxioms.lean
\end{verbatim}
When the unconditional coverage theorem is installed, the final axiom check is:
\begin{verbatim}
lean Cuboctahedron/PrintAxioms.lean
\end{verbatim}
