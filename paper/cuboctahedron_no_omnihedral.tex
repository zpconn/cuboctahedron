\begin{abstract}
We describe a Lean-checkable reduction of the following problem: a
cuboctahedron has no nonsingular periodic billiard trajectory that meets each
of its fourteen faces exactly once in a period.  The proof uses only exact
rational arithmetic.  A billiard trajectory is unfolded to a straight line in
a reflected development, and a cyclic starting convention reduces the search
to \(97{,}297{,}200\) started words in opposite-face pairs, with six sign bits
in the translation case.

The first structural ingredient is a holonomy normal form.  It records the
parity of the three square-pair reflections and a reduced projective word in
the four triangular directions.  For every valid started pair word \(w\), the
linear return map is the identity if and only if this reduced triangular
shadow is empty.  This theorem is proved in Lean by reducing integer-scaled
triangular reflection products modulo \(3\).

The second ingredient, and the main proof-engineering strategy for the
remaining finite coverage, is Bellman certification.  Instead of enumerating
individual ranks in the nonidentity residual branch, a generated Lean module
defines a semantic language of forced-axis cases, a finite transition system
with integer gains, and an integer potential \(V\).  Lean checks the local
inequalities
\[
  g(e)+V(\operatorname{dst}(e))\le V(\operatorname{src}(e)),
\]
and the usual telescoping argument proves that every accepted word in the
language has nonpositive obstruction margin.  Since feasibility would require
that margin to be positive, the whole semantic family is killed at once.
External scripts may find the language, gains, and potential, but Lean checks
the transition closure, the potential inequalities, and the geometric
interpretation of the margin.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Periodic billiards in polyhedra are much less rigid than periodic billiards in
rational polygons.  Even for a highly symmetric polyhedron, an orbit may
reflect through many spatial copies before returning.  The question studied
here is deliberately small but sharp: can a cuboctahedron have a nonsingular
periodic orbit that hits all fourteen faces exactly once?

\begin{theorem}[Target theorem]
The cuboctahedron has no nonsingular periodic billiard orbit that meets each
of its fourteen faces exactly once in one period.
\end{theorem}

Here nonsingular means that no impact lies on an edge or vertex, and
``meets each face exactly once'' is meant during one period.  The point of the
present project is not only to search for obstructions, but to organize the
obstruction into a formal proof that can be checked by Lean 4 and mathlib.
This imposes two practical requirements.  First, all geometric computations
must be exact.  Floating point evidence is useful for discovery, but it is not
proof evidence.  Second, the finite enumeration must be exposed to Lean by
small soundness theorems and certificate checkers, not by trusting a large
external program.

The formal proof has three layers.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item The geometric layer defines the cuboctahedron, its face reflections,
  unfolded billiard witnesses, and the reduction to started face itineraries.
\item The structural layer proves a holonomy normal form that classifies the
  linear part of the return map from a pair word alone.
\item The finite layer kills the two resulting branches.  The identity branch
  is a translation problem in two variables, handled by GoodDirection and
  Farkas-type certificates.  The nonidentity residual branch is organized by
  Bellman-style potential certificates over semantic word languages.
\end{enumerate}

The public Lean theorem currently has the finite generated coverage as an
explicit hypothesis:
\begin{verbatim}
theorem Cuboctahedron.conditional_cuboctahedron_no_omnihedral
    (coverage : ExhaustiveGeneratedCoverage) :
    Not (exists o : BilliardOrbit14,
      o.Nonsingular /\ o.Periodic /\ o.TouchesEachFaceExactlyOnce)
\end{verbatim}
Thus the handwritten bridge from complete coverage to the billiard theorem is
already formal.  The remaining task is to provide coverage in a form Lean can
check without changing the theorem being proved.

\section{The Exact Cuboctahedron Model}

We use the rational coordinate model
\[
P=\{(x,y,z)\in\R^3:
 |x|\le 1,\ |y|\le 1,\ |z|\le 1,\
 \pm x\pm y\pm z\le 2\}.
\]
The six square faces are
\[
X_\pm:\ \pm x=1,\qquad
Y_\pm:\ \pm y=1,\qquad
Z_\pm:\ \pm z=1,
\]
and the eight triangular faces are
\[
T_{\epsilon_1\epsilon_2\epsilon_3}:
\epsilon_1x+\epsilon_2y+\epsilon_3z=2,
\qquad \epsilon_i\in\{\pm1\}.
\]
Every face lies in a plane
\[
  n_F\dotp p=c_F
\]
with \(n_F\in\Q^3\) and \(c_F\in\Q\).  Reflection in this plane is the affine
map
\[
  r_F(p)=p-2\,\frac{n_F\dotp p-c_F}{n_F\dotp n_F}\,n_F.
\]
Consequently every reflection matrix and translation vector used by the proof
has rational entries.

\begin{definition}[Opposite-face pairs]
Two faces are in the same opposite-face pair if their supporting planes are
parallel.  The seven pairs are denoted
\[
x,\ y,\ z,\ d_{111},\ d_{11\bar 1},\ d_{1\bar 1 1},\
d_{\bar 1 11}.
\]
The first three are square pairs.  The last four are triangular pairs, named
by projective normal direction.
\end{definition}

Opposite faces have the same linear reflection part.  They differ only in the
translation term of the affine reflection.  This is why the proof first
classifies pair words and introduces signs only when the affine data matter.

\section{Unfolding and Started Words}

The standard unfolding construction replaces a reflected billiard path by a
straight line through reflected copies of the polyhedron.

\begin{definition}[Face itinerary]
A period-14 face itinerary is a function
\[
  s:\{0,\ldots,13\}\to\F.
\]
It is \emph{omnihedral} if it is a bijection onto the fourteen faces.  It is
\emph{started} if \(s(0)=X_+\).
\end{definition}

For an itinerary \(s\), define the affine return map
\[
  A_s=r_{s(13)}\circ\cdots\circ r_{s(0)},\qquad
  A_s(p)=M_s p+b_s.
\]
The order convention in the Lean code is the corresponding left-to-right
product of reflection matrices in the order crossed by the unfolded line.

\begin{definition}[Unfolded witness]
An \emph{unfolded witness} for \(s\) is a line
\[
  \ell(t)=p+tv,\qquad v\ne0,
\]
such that the developed line crosses the reflected copy of \(s(i)\) at the
\(i\)-th impact, each crossing lies in the relative interior of the developed
face, and \(A_s\) maps the line to itself with direction \(v\).  In
particular,
\[
  M_s v=v.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Unfolding criterion]
A nonsingular billiard orbit with face itinerary \(s\) exists if and only if
there is an unfolded witness for \(s\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Reflect the next copy of \(P\) across the impact face rather than reflecting
the velocity vector.  The incoming and outgoing billiard segments become one
straight segment in the reflected development.  The relative-interior
condition is unchanged by reflection and is exactly nonsingularity.  The
periodicity condition says that the affine return map identifies the final
developed line with the initial one.  The construction is reversible by
folding the reflected copies back into \(P\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Starting-face reduction]
If a nonsingular omnihedral period-14 orbit exists, then there is one whose
itinerary is started.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
An omnihedral period hits \(X_+\) exactly once.  Cyclically reindex the period
so that this hit is first.
\end{proof}

It remains to rule out started unfolded witnesses.

\begin{definition}[Started pair word]
A \emph{started pair word} is a length-13 word in the seven opposite-face
pairs, recording the opposite-face pair of each face after the initial
\(X_+\).  Such a word is \emph{valid} if the multiplicities are
\[
x:1,\qquad y:2,\qquad z:2,\qquad
d_{111},d_{11\bar 1},d_{1\bar 1 1},d_{\bar 1 11}:2.
\]
The finite set of valid started pair words is denoted \(\W\).
\end{definition}

The cardinality is
\[
  |\W|=\frac{13!}{2^6}=97{,}297{,}200.
\]
Lean defines a lexicographic map
\[
  \unrank:\{0,\ldots,97{,}297{,}199\}\to\W
\]
and proves that it is a bijection.  Ranks are addresses, not a mathematical
compression coordinate.

In the identity-linear branch the proof also keeps track of the choices
inside opposite-face pairs.  Once \(X_+\) is fixed, the six remaining
opposite-face signs are independent.  We write
\[
  \Masks=\{\pm1\}^6
\]
for the sign-mask set.

\section{The Holonomy Normal Form}

For a valid pair word \(w\), let \(M(w)\) be the linear part of the unfolded
return map obtained by using the pair reflection matrices.  This matrix
depends only on \(w\), not on the sign mask.

The pair-word product convention is
\[
  R(w_0)R(w_1)\cdots R(w_{12})R(x),
\]
where the final \(R(x)\) is the reflection associated to the initial started
face \(X_+\).

\begin{definition}[Square parity]
The square parity is an element
\[
  p=(p_x,p_y,p_z)\in(\Z/2\Z)^3.
\]
Scanning a square-pair letter toggles the corresponding coordinate of \(p\).
Scanning a triangular-pair letter leaves \(p\) unchanged.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Triangular shadow]
Let
\[
  \T=\{111,\ 11\bar 1,\ 1\bar 1 1,\ \bar 1 11\}
\]
be the four projective triangular directions.  When a triangular letter is
scanned after accumulated square parity \(p\), it is conjugated by \(p\).  If
the letter is written projectively as \((1,\eta,\zeta)\), then \(p\) sends it
to
\[
  \bigl(1,\ (-1)^{p_x+p_y}\eta,\ (-1)^{p_x+p_z}\zeta\bigr).
\]
The triangular shadow is a stack of such acted letters, with adjacent equal
letters cancelled.  The final stack is denoted \(\red(w)\).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Holonomy classifier]
For every valid started pair word \(w\),
\[
  M(w)=I
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \red(w)=[].
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The scanner maintains a product invariant.  The accumulated pair-reflection
product factors as a triangular product times the square-parity matrix.  The
valid multiplicities imply that after the final started \(x\) factor is
included, the square parity is trivial.

Adjacent equal triangular reflections square to the identity, so stack
reduction does not change the triangular product.  It remains to show that a
nonempty reduced triangular word cannot have identity product.

For a triangular normal \(n\in\{\pm1\}^3\), the reflection matrix is
\[
  H_n=I-\frac{2}{3}nn^T.
\]
Scale it to the integer matrix
\[
  K_n=3H_n=3I-2nn^T.
\]
Modulo \(3\), one has \(K_n\equiv nn^T\).  A nonempty reduced triangular word
therefore gives, modulo \(3\), a product of rank-one matrices.  Since adjacent
projective triangular letters are distinct, each successive dot product is
nonzero in \(\mathbb F_3\).  The product is a nonzero scalar multiple of an
outer product, hence is nonzero modulo \(3\).  If the rational triangular
product were \(I\), the scaled integer product of length \(m>0\) would be
\(3^mI\), which is zero modulo \(3\), a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
An exact external scan found \(2{,}468{,}088\) empty reduced shadows and
\(94{,}829{,}112\) nonempty reduced shadows.  These numbers are useful
diagnostics, but they are not used as proof.  The Lean theorem above is the
trusted identity/nonidentity split.
\end{remark}

\section{The Two Geometric Branches}

\subsection{Nonidentity holonomy}

Assume \(\red(w)\ne[]\), so \(M(w)\ne I\).  If an unfolded witness exists for
a signed itinerary realizing \(w\), then the return map \(A(p)=Mp+b\) carries
the witness line to itself.

\begin{lemma}[Forced-axis equation]
Let \(A(p)=Mp+b\) with \(M\ne I\).  If \(A\) preserves the line
\(p_0+\R v\) and preserves its direction, then \(v\ne0\), \(Mv=v\), and there
is \(\lambda\in\R\) such that
\[
  (I-M)p_0=b-\lambda v.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The direction condition gives \(Mv=v\).  Since \(A(p_0)\) lies on the same
line, \(Mp_0+b=p_0+\lambda v\) for some \(\lambda\), which is the displayed
equation.
\end{proof}

In many nonidentity cases this forced-axis geometry immediately contradicts
nonsingularity: there is no fixed direction, the forced axis misses the
relative interior of \(X_+\), the crossing direction has the wrong sign, or a
forced impact falls outside the intended face.

The difficult residual cases are those for which the elementary forced-axis
checks leave a family of signed face sequences.  For these cases the proof
uses an exact rational \emph{margin} \(\mu\).  The geometric construction of
\(\mu\) depends on the family, but its role is uniform:
\[
  \text{every feasible unfolded witness in the family would force } \mu>0.
\]
A certificate killing the family is therefore allowed to prove the opposite
inequality \(\mu\le0\).  The Bellman strategy described in the next section is
the current route for proving this inequality for whole semantic families at
once.

\subsection{Identity holonomy}

Assume \(\red(w)=[]\), so \(M(w)=I\).  The affine return map is a translation
\[
  A(p)=p+b.
\]
For a nondegenerate periodic unfolded witness, the line direction is parallel
to \(b\).  Since the first face is \(X_+\), write the starting point as
\[
  p_0=(1,y,z).
\]
For a fixed pair word and sign mask, the face-interior and crossing-order
conditions become strict rational linear inequalities in \(y\) and \(z\).

If the \(i\)-th developed face plane is
\[
  u_i\dotp p=d_i,
\]
then the crossing time along direction \(b\) is
\[
  t_i=\frac{d_i-u_i\dotp p_0}{u_i\dotp b}.
\]
The denominator \(D_i=u_i\dotp b\) must have the correct sign for every
internal crossing.

\begin{definition}[GoodDirection]
An identity-linear signed case satisfies \emph{GoodDirection} if all internal
crossing denominators \(D_i\) are positive in the chosen orientation.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[GoodDirection is necessary]
If an identity-linear signed itinerary has an unfolded witness, then it
satisfies GoodDirection.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Each prescribed developed face must be crossed in forward time and from the
prescribed side.  With the fixed orientation, the numerator in the formula for
\(t_i\) has the prescribed sign.  A nonpositive denominator would reverse or
degenerate that crossing.
\end{proof}

After GoodDirection, the remaining translation constraints are strict rational
linear inequalities
\[
  a_jy+c_jz+e_j>0.
\]
Lean checks infeasibility with a strict two-variable Farkas theorem: if
nonnegative rational multipliers \(q_j\), not all zero, satisfy
\[
  \sum_j q_ja_j=0,\qquad
  \sum_j q_jc_j=0,\qquad
  \sum_j q_je_j\le0,
\]
then the strict system has no solution.  Source-indexed rows record the
geometric origin of each inequality, so a generated Farkas certificate names
the intended row source and Lean checks both row production and the rational
linear combination.

\section{Bellman Certificates}

The Bellman strategy is a way to turn many local nonidentity residual cases
into one small theorem about paths in a finite transition system.  It is
separate from the cuboctahedron geometry: the Lean module
\texttt{Cuboctahedron.Search.BellmanPotential} contains only integer gains,
paths, and potentials.  Geometry enters later by constructing a semantic
language whose accepted words describe forced-axis objects and by proving that
the Bellman margin is the geometric obstruction margin for those objects.

\begin{definition}[Bellman graph]
Let \(S\) be a finite or finitely presented state type.  A Bellman edge is a
triple
\[
  e=(s,g,t),\qquad s,t\in S,\quad g\in\Z.
\]
Here \(s\) is the source state, \(t\) is the destination state, and \(g\) is
the integer gain attached to the transition.  A potential is a function
\[
  V:S\to\Z.
\]
The edge \(e=(s,g,t)\) is \emph{\(V\)-valid} if
\[
  g+V(t)\le V(s).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Bellman telescoping]
Let
\[
  s_0\xrightarrow{g_0}s_1\xrightarrow{g_1}\cdots
  \xrightarrow{g_{m-1}}s_m
\]
be a path all of whose edges are \(V\)-valid.  Then
\[
  \sum_{i=0}^{m-1}g_i+V(s_m)\le V(s_0).
\]
If \(V(s_m)\ge0\), then
\[
  \sum_{i=0}^{m-1}g_i\le V(s_0).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Sum the inequalities \(g_i+V(s_{i+1})\le V(s_i)\).  All intermediate
potential terms cancel.
\end{proof}

The Lean theorem is stated in several equivalent forms: unlabeled paths,
graph paths carrying a proof of graph membership, labeled graph paths, labeled
runs, and label-step runs.  The most production-facing form uses labels and a
step relation.

\begin{definition}[Label-step run]
Let \(L\) be a label type and let
\[
  \operatorname{Step}(s,\ell,t,g)
\]
be a proposition saying that label \(\ell\) can move state \(s\) to state
\(t\) with gain \(g\).  A label-step run over a word
\(\ell_0,\ldots,\ell_{m-1}\) is a chain
\[
  s_0\xrightarrow{\ell_0,g_0}s_1
  \xrightarrow{\ell_1,g_1}\cdots
  \xrightarrow{\ell_{m-1},g_{m-1}}s_m
\]
whose transitions satisfy \(\operatorname{Step}\).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Language Bellman certificate]
Fix a start state \(s_0\), a constant \(c\in\Z\), a potential \(V:S\to\Z\),
a step relation, a word map \(\word:X\to L^\ast\), an acceptance predicate
\(\operatorname{Accepts}\) on \(X\), and an integer-valued scaled margin
\(\mu:X\to\Z\).  Suppose:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item every accepted object \(x\) has a label-step run over \(\word(x)\), with
  final state \(s_f\) and total gain \(G\);
\item the final potential is nonnegative: \(V(s_f)\ge0\);
\item the margin is bounded by the path gain: \(\mu(x)\le c+G\);
\item every step satisfies the local potential inequality
  \(g+V(t)\le V(s)\);
\item the root bound \(c+V(s_0)\le0\) holds.
\end{enumerate}
Then every accepted object has nonpositive scaled margin:
\[
  \operatorname{Accepts}(x)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mu(x)\le0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For an accepted \(x\), apply Bellman telescoping to its run.  The local
potential inequalities and \(V(s_f)\ge0\) give \(G\le V(s_0)\).  Hence
\[
  \mu(x)\le c+G\le c+V(s_0)\le0.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{How Bellman enters the nonidentity proof}

The residual nonidentity families are naturally words in a small alphabet of
signed faces and opposite-face pairs.  A typical Bellman family is specified
by semantic conditions such as:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item a cancellation-pairing pattern in the reduced triangular shadow;
\item a schedule restricting which signed faces may occur at each contribution
  step;
\item a square-gap condition recording how square faces are distributed among
  triangular contributions;
\item local-axis sign conditions checked by exact prefix reflection matrices;
\item a canonical bad face whose start-interior margin is being tested.
\end{enumerate}

These conditions define a \emph{closed language}: if a word is accepted, every
transition needed by the Bellman certificate stays inside the finite state
graph for the family.  The state is not the raw rank and not the full face
prefix.  The accepted coordinate in the current diagnostics records the step
together with triangular source-position progress; this is fine enough to
exclude spurious positive paths and coarse enough to avoid replaying
individual ranks.

For such a family, the generated data have the following proof obligations.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item Define the label type, state type, step relation, potential \(V\), root
  constant \(c\), and scaled margin \(\mu\), all as exact integers.
\item Prove each local step inequality \(g+V(t)\le V(s)\).
\item Prove that every word in the semantic language constructs a label-step
  run with the advertised total gain.
\item Prove that the generated margin agrees with the rational geometric
  margin after clearing denominators.
\item Prove that \(\mu\le0\) contradicts the strict positive margin required
  by a feasible forced-axis witness.
\end{enumerate}

The external program may discover the graph and the potential, but it is not
trusted.  Lean checks the closed language, all edge inequalities, and the
geometric bridge from nonpositive margin to impossibility.

\begin{remark}[Why this is not rank enumeration]
A rank is still used to address a pair word when the final exhaustive theorem
is assembled.  The Bellman certificate itself is deliberately stated over a
semantic language.  A single potential proof can kill all ranks whose
forced-axis data land in that language.  This is the main reason for using
Bellman certificates: the proof object scales with the automaton and the
semantic family, not with a contiguous interval of lexicographic ranks.
\end{remark}

\section{Generated Coverage}

The final finite theorem packages the two branches into semantic predicates.
In Lean these public predicates have the shape
\begin{center}
\texttt{NonIdentityRankKilled r}\\
\texttt{TranslationCaseKilled r mask}
\end{center}
The first means that if rank \(r\) is nonidentity-linear, then no signed
started itinerary realizing it has an unfolded witness.  The second means that
if rank \(r\) is identity-linear, then the signed translation case indexed by
the mask has no unfolded witness.

\begin{definition}[Semantic generated coverage]
Semantic generated coverage consists of:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item the verified rank/unrank address space for all \(97{,}297{,}200\)
  started pair words;
\item the verified \(64\)-element sign-mask address space;
\item a proof of \texttt{NonIdentityRankKilled r} for every rank \(r\);
\item a proof of \texttt{TranslationCaseKilled r mask} for every rank and
  mask.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Coverage kills started witnesses]
If semantic generated coverage holds, then no started omnihedral itinerary
has an unfolded witness.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let a started unfolded witness be given.  Forgetting signs gives a valid
started pair word \(w\), hence a rank \(r=\rank(w)\).  By the holonomy
classifier, either \(\red(w)\ne[]\) and the case is nonidentity-linear, or
\(\red(w)=[]\) and the case is identity-linear.

In the nonidentity case, \texttt{NonIdentityRankKilled r} contradicts the
witness.  In the identity case, the itinerary determines a sign mask \(m\),
and \texttt{TranslationCaseKilled r m} contradicts the witness.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Conditional formal theorem]
If semantic generated coverage holds, then the cuboctahedron has no
nonsingular periodic billiard orbit that meets each face exactly once.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume such an orbit exists.  Cyclically reindex it to start at \(X_+\).  The
unfolding criterion gives a started unfolded witness.  This contradicts the
previous proposition.
\end{proof}

\section{Verification Boundary}

The trusted part of the project is the Lean development.  It includes:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item exact rational definitions of the cuboctahedron, faces, and reflection
  maps;
\item the unfolding criterion and started-itinerary reduction;
\item the rank/unrank enumeration theorem for valid pair words;
\item the holonomy normal form and identity classifier;
\item GoodDirection necessity and strict Farkas soundness for translation
  cases;
\item the generic Bellman potential theorems;
\item generated Lean modules that check concrete semantic coverage
  certificates.
\end{enumerate}

The untrusted part consists of profilers, search scripts, JSON files, markdown
reports, and old C++ or Python outputs.  These artifacts are allowed to
suggest certificate data.  They become proof only when Lean checks a theorem
whose statement is strong enough to contribute to semantic coverage.

The formal proof code is constrained to exact arithmetic.  The final theorem
must not rely on floating-point arithmetic, numerical tolerances, custom
axioms, \texttt{sorry}, \texttt{admit}, \texttt{unsafe}, or native-code
evaluation.
